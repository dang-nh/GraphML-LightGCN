\begin{abstract}
LightGCL (ICLR 2023) is a graph contrastive learning model for recommendation that
contrasts a LightGCN view of the user--item graph with an SVD-reconstructed view, yet its
ranking objective still relies on \emph{uniformly} sampled negative items in the BPR loss.
We propose a degree-aware curriculum hard-negative sampler for this BPR component:
negatives are drawn from a mixture of a uniform distribution and a Personalized-PageRank
(PPR) hard-negative distribution, whose mixture weight grows with training progress
(curriculum) and with each user's activity level (degree), so that sparse users keep
mostly random negatives, limiting false-negative bias. On Yelp2018, a fixed-ramp mixture
(S2) and our degree-gated curriculum mixture (S3) both improve over the uniform baseline
(S0) and over pure PPR hard negatives (S1) on Recall@20/NDCG@20 and on every degree
bucket (head/mid/tail), consistently across 3 seeds; however, S3's extra degree-gating
does not show a measurable additional benefit over the simpler S2 in our experiments,
including in the tail bucket where it was expected to matter most. SimGCL, an external
contrastive-learning baseline, outperforms all LightGCL variants tested. We report all
numbers honestly, including this partly-negative finding on the core research question.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Graph-based collaborative filtering models such as LightGCN~\cite{lightgcn} and its
contrastive-learning successor LightGCL~\cite{lightgcl} rank items for a user via an
inner product of learned embeddings, trained with the pairwise BPR loss~\cite{bpr}. BPR
requires, for every observed (user, item) interaction, a sampled \emph{negative} item
that the user is assumed not to prefer. In nearly all implementations, including
LightGCL's official code, this negative is drawn \emph{uniformly at random} from the
user's non-interacted items. On sparse implicit-feedback graphs this is a reasonable
default but a known missed opportunity: most uniformly-sampled negatives are "easy"
(unrelated to the user's taste) and contribute little gradient signal, while a small
number of \emph{hard} negatives --- items structurally close to the user in the
interaction graph but never interacted with --- would push the model harder. The risk of
hard-negative mining is \emph{false-negative bias}: an item that looks hard precisely
because it is popular among the user's neighborhood is disproportionately likely to be an
unobserved \emph{positive}, especially for users with very few training interactions,
where the model has the least evidence to tell the two apart.

\textbf{Research question.} Does a hard-negative curriculum modulated by user activity
improve the recommendation accuracy and long-tail robustness of LightGCL without
increasing false-negative bias?

We answer this with a controlled ablation, changing \emph{only} the BPR negative sampler
of the official LightGCL implementation and leaving its SVD contrastive view and
InfoNCE loss untouched, then compare four sampler variants (uniform, pure hard-negative,
fixed-ramp mixture, and degree-gated curriculum mixture) against each other and against
three external baselines (MF-BPR, LightGCN, SimGCL) on Yelp2018.

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}

Our contribution sits at the intersection of two lines of research: negative
sampling for implicit-feedback recommendation, and graph contrastive learning
for collaborative filtering. We review each and then position the degree-gated
curriculum precisely against both.

\subsection{Negative sampling and hard-negative mining for recommendation}
Implicit-feedback recommenders observe only positive interactions; the pairwise
BPR objective~\cite{bpr} therefore requires a \emph{negative} item for every
observed pair, conventionally drawn uniformly from the non-interacted set. Because
the overwhelming majority of uniform negatives are trivially easy---items with no
structural or semantic relationship to the user---they saturate the sigmoid early
and contribute vanishing gradient, which slows convergence and caps final ranking
quality. A long line of work has therefore sought \emph{informative} negatives.

\textbf{Dynamic and score-based negatives.} Dynamic Negative Sampling
(DNS)~\cite{dns} was the first to argue that the negatives most useful for
top-$N$ ranking are those the current model scores highly but that are in fact
unobserved: for each positive it oversamples a small candidate set and keeps the
highest-scoring (``hardest'') item, adaptively raising negative difficulty as the
model improves. This score-conditioned view was later formalized as
adversarial sampling. IRGAN~\cite{irgan} casts a generator against the
recommender in a minimax game so that the generator learns to propose negatives
the discriminator finds confusing. Both establish the central tension that our
work also confronts: harder negatives sharpen the decision boundary but push the
sampler toward unobserved positives (false negatives), which are precisely the
high-scoring items a well-trained model surfaces.

\textbf{False negatives and robust sampling.} SRNS~\cite{srns} made the
false-negative problem explicit, observing empirically that true hard negatives
and false negatives are separable by prediction \emph{variance}: false negatives
(unobserved positives) receive stably high scores across epochs, whereas genuine
hard negatives fluctuate. SRNS therefore favours high-variance candidates from a
small memory. Our degree gate targets the same failure mode but through a
different, cheaper signal---\emph{user activity}---on the premise that a
PPR-proximate item is far more likely to be an unobserved positive for a sparse
user (little evidence, high graph overlap between liked and merely-adjacent items)
than for an active one.

\textbf{Graph-structure-aware and mixed negatives.} On GNN recommenders,
MixGCF~\cite{mixgcf} generates \emph{synthetic} hard negatives by (i) positive
mixing---interpolating a positive's embedding into sampled negatives---and (ii)
hop mixing across the GNN's aggregation layers, rather than selecting existing
items; it reports consistent gains over DNS on LightGCN and NGCF. Most directly
relevant to our design, PinSAGE~\cite{pinsage} pioneered \emph{PPR-based} hard
negatives at web scale: it adds, per positive, ``hard'' items drawn from an
intermediate band of the query node's Personalized-PageRank ranking---close enough
to be informative, far enough to remain true negatives. We adopt exactly this
PPR-band construction as our hard-negative distribution. Finally, in the adjacent
field of dense text retrieval, ANCE~\cite{ance} showed that globally-mined,
periodically-refreshed nearest-neighbour negatives substantially outperform
in-batch negatives, reinforcing that the \emph{source} of negatives, not only the
loss, governs contrastive-style training quality.

\subsection{Graph contrastive learning for collaborative filtering}
A parallel line augments graph collaborative filtering with a self-supervised
contrastive objective. SGL~\cite{sgl} generates two augmented views of the
user--item graph via edge/node dropout and random walks and maximizes agreement
between a node's representations across views with an InfoNCE loss, improving
robustness and---notably for us---long-tail accuracy. NCL~\cite{ncl} enriches the
contrastive signal with structural and semantic neighbours (prototype- and
graph-neighbour-based positives) rather than relying on stochastic augmentation.
SimGCL~\cite{simgcl} then showed that the graph augmentations themselves are
largely unnecessary: injecting small isotropic noise into the embedding space
yields more uniform representations and stronger performance than dropout-based
views, a finding consistent with the strong SimGCL numbers we observe
(Table~\ref{tab:main-results}). LightGCL~\cite{lightgcl}, our base model, replaces
stochastic augmentation with a deterministic, global view built from a truncated
SVD of the normalized adjacency, aligning the LightGCN view with the SVD view via
InfoNCE~\cite{infonce} and thereby injecting global collaborative signal that local
propagation misses.

\subsection{Positioning of this work}
Two observations motivate our specific design. First, essentially all of the
contrastive recommenders above---SGL, NCL, SimGCL, and LightGCL---inherit a
\emph{uniform} BPR negative sampler for their supervised ranking term and direct
their innovation entirely at the self-supervised branch; the ranking term's
negatives are left untouched. Second, essentially all of the hard-negative methods
above---DNS, IRGAN, SRNS, MixGCF---were developed for non-contrastive
recommenders and apply a \emph{single}, user-agnostic notion of hardness. Our work
occupies the gap between these two literatures: we keep LightGCL's SVD-contrastive
branch fixed and instead upgrade only its BPR negative sampler, and we make the
hardness schedule \emph{two-dimensional}---a curriculum ramp over training
time~\cite{curriculum} \emph{and} a degree gate over user activity. To our
knowledge this specific combination---degree-conditioned modulation of a
PPR-hard-negative curriculum, applied to the supervised component of an
SVD-contrastive model---has not been studied. The degree gate is the conceptual
novelty relative to a plain curriculum mixture (our S2 baseline) and to
uniform-hardness methods like DNS/PinSAGE: it encodes the prior that the
false-negative cost of hard negatives is not constant across users but scales
inversely with the evidence the model has about each user.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{From maximum-likelihood ranking to the BPR loss}
Users $u$ and items $i$ are embedded as $e_u, e_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$, scored by inner
product $\hat y_{ui} = e_u^\top e_i$. We restate the BPR objective~\cite{bpr} as a
maximum-a-posteriori estimator to make explicit which quantity the negative sampler
perturbs. Let $\Theta$ collect all model parameters and let $\succ_u\;\subseteq I\times I$
denote user $u$'s latent total order over items. BPR assumes each user's pairwise
preferences are drawn independently and that, for an observed item $i$ and a non-observed
item $j$, the model's belief that $u$ prefers $i$ to $j$ is
\[
p(i \succ_u j \mid \Theta) \;=\; \sigma\!\big(\hat y_{ui}(\Theta) - \hat y_{uj}(\Theta)\big),
\qquad \sigma(x)=\tfrac{1}{1+e^{-x}}.
\]
With a factorized Gaussian prior $p(\Theta)=\mathcal{N}(0,\lambda_2^{-1}I)$, the
posterior over parameters given the observed preferences $D_S=\{(u,i,j): i\in N_u,\,
j\notin N_u\}$ factorizes, and maximizing its log yields the BPR-OPT criterion
\[
\max_{\Theta}\;\sum_{(u,i,j)\in D_S}\log\sigma(\hat y_{ui}-\hat y_{uj})
\;-\;\lambda_2\lVert\Theta\rVert_2^2 .
\]
Because $D_S$ is quadratically large, it is approximated by sampling one negative
$j$ per observed pair, giving the stochastic loss we minimize,
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bpr}}
= \sum_{(u,i)\in O}\;\mathbb{E}_{j\sim P(\cdot\mid u)}
\big[-\log\sigma(\hat y_{ui}-\hat y_{uj})\big]
\;+\;\lambda_2\lVert\Theta\rVert_2^2 ,
\]
where $P(\cdot\mid u)$ is uniform over non-interacted items in all baselines. This
derivation clarifies our intervention: the \emph{estimator} is fixed; we change only the
proposal distribution $P(\cdot\mid u)$ from which the Monte-Carlo negative $j$ is drawn ---
the only component we modify. The gradient with respect to a score margin
$x=\hat y_{ui}-\hat y_{uj}$ is $-\sigma(-x)$, which vanishes as $x\!\to\!\infty$:
uniformly-sampled easy negatives quickly reach large $x$ and stop contributing,
motivating harder proposals. Symmetrically, if $j$ is in fact an unobserved
positive, the same gradient actively pushes a good item down --- the false-negative
cost the degree gate is designed to bound.

\subsection{LightGCN propagation and the LightGCL SVD-contrastive objective}
\label{sec:lightgcl-formal}
\textbf{Graph propagation.} Let $A\in\{0,1\}^{|U|\times|I|}$ be the interaction
matrix and $\tilde{\mathcal{A}}=\mathcal{D}^{-1/2}\mathcal{A}\mathcal{D}^{-1/2}$
the symmetric-normalized adjacency of the bipartite graph
$\mathcal{A}=\big[\begin{smallmatrix}0&A\\A^\top&0\end{smallmatrix}\big]$, with
degree diagonal $\mathcal{D}$. LightGCN~\cite{lightgcn} discards nonlinearities
and per-layer weights, propagating embeddings by
\[
E^{(l+1)}=\tilde{\mathcal{A}}\,E^{(l)},\qquad
E=\frac{1}{L+1}\sum_{l=0}^{L}E^{(l)},
\]
where $E^{(0)}$ are the free embeddings and $E$ the final read-out used for
scoring $\hat y_{ui}=e_u^\top e_i$. Layer averaging acts as a low-pass filter over
the graph spectrum, mitigating oversmoothing at moderate $L$; we use $L=2$.

\textbf{SVD-reconstructed contrastive view.} LightGCL~\cite{lightgcl} builds a
second view from a rank-$q$ truncated SVD $\tilde{\mathcal{A}}\approx
U_q\Sigma_q V_q^{\top}$, and propagates embeddings over this low-rank
reconstruction to obtain a global-signal view $G^{(l)}$. The two views are aligned
with an InfoNCE~\cite{infonce} objective using in-batch negatives; writing
$z^{(1)}_u,z^{(2)}_u$ for the main- and SVD-view representations of user $u$ in a
batch $\mathcal{B}$ and $\tau$ for the temperature,
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cl}}^{U}
= \sum_{u\in\mathcal{B}} -\log
\frac{\exp\!\big(z^{(1)\top}_u z^{(2)}_u/\tau\big)}
{\sum_{u'\in\mathcal{B}}\exp\!\big(z^{(1)\top}_u z^{(2)}_{u'}/\tau\big)},
\]
with an analogous item term $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cl}}^{I}$, added to
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bpr}}$ with weight $\lambda_1$:
\[
\mathcal{L}=\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bpr}}
+\lambda_1\big(\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cl}}^{U}+\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cl}}^{I}\big)
+\lambda_2\lVert\Theta\rVert_2^2 .
\]
Critically, we verified in the official code (\texttt{model.py}) that the ranking
score $\hat y_{ui}$ is read only from the main branch $E$; the SVD view enters
exclusively through $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cl}}$. This means LightGCN and MF-BPR
baselines can be obtained for free as ablations of the same codebase
(\S\ref{sec:setup}), and that our sampler change --- which only affects
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bpr}}$'s negative $j$ --- provably cannot alter the
contrastive branch at all, the property that makes this a clean, single-variable
ablation.

\subsection{Proposed: degree-aware curriculum hard-negative sampling}
\textbf{PPR hard-negative band.} For each user $u$ we compute Personalized
PageRank on $\tilde{\mathcal{A}}$ with teleport $c$, solving the fixed point
$\pi_u=(1-c)\tilde{\mathcal{A}}\pi_u+c\,e_u$ by power iteration (Algorithm~\ref{alg:ppr}),
following PinSAGE~\cite{pinsage}. Ranking all items by $\pi_u$ and excluding observed
positives $N_u$, the hard-negative candidate band is the percentile window
\[
B_u=\big\{\,j\notin N_u \;:\; \mathrm{rank}_{\mathrm{PPR}}(j\mid u)\in
[p_{lo}\,|I|,\;p_{hi}\,|I|]\,\big\},
\qquad P_{\mathrm{PPR}}(j\mid u)=\mathrm{Unif}(B_u).
\]
The lower cutoff $p_{lo}$ deliberately discards the very top of the ranking, where
unobserved positives concentrate; the band width $p_{hi}-p_{lo}$ trades
informativeness (narrow, close to the top) against false-negative safety (wide,
further down). We downsample $B_u$ once to a fixed pool of $\mathrm{POOL}=500$ items
per user for $O(1)$ online sampling.

\textbf{Two-factor mixture weight.} At epoch $t$ the negative for user $u$ is drawn
from a per-user, per-epoch mixture of the uniform and PPR distributions,
\[
P_t(j\mid u)=(1-\alpha_{u,t})\,P_{\mathrm{uniform}}(j)
+\alpha_{u,t}\,P_{\mathrm{PPR}}(j\mid u),
\]
\[
\alpha_{u,t}=\underbrace{s(t)}_{\text{curriculum}}\cdot
\underbrace{\sigma\!\big(a\,[\log(1+d_u)-\log(1+d_{\mathrm{mid}})]\big)}_{\text{degree gate}},
\qquad s(t)=\min\!\Big(1,\tfrac{t}{T_w}\Big).
\]
The curriculum term $s(t)$ ramps hard negatives from $0$ to full over a $T_w$-epoch
warm-up~\cite{curriculum}, so early training --- when the model cannot yet
distinguish hard negatives from false negatives --- sees mostly uniform negatives.
The degree gate is a sigmoid in \emph{log}-degree, pinned to $\alpha=0.5$ at the
midpoint degree $d_{\mathrm{mid}}$ (set to the median) and with slope $a$: active
users ($d_u\gg d_{\mathrm{mid}}$) receive hard negatives approaching probability
$1$, while sparse users ($d_u\ll d_{\mathrm{mid}}$) are protected toward uniform
sampling. We compare four sampler modes, all applied only to LightGCL's BPR term,
each a special case of the expression above:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{S0} --- uniform (the unmodified baseline): $\alpha_{u,t}\equiv 0$.
\item \textbf{S1} --- pure PPR hard negatives ($\alpha_{u,t}\equiv 1$, no curriculum/gate).
\item \textbf{S2} --- fixed global mixture ($\alpha_{u,t} = s(t)\cdot\bar\alpha$, no degree
dependence).
\item \textbf{S3} --- our full method: degree-gated curriculum mixture, as above.
\end{itemize}
Algorithm~\ref{alg:sampler} gives the fully-vectorized per-epoch sampling procedure,
which adds $<\!1$s/epoch and no per-step cost since it operates once per epoch over
all $|O|$ training edges rather than per gradient step. Full implementation details
are in \texttt{docs/design/sampler\_design.md} and \texttt{scripts/precompute\_ppr.py};
PPR is precomputed once per dataset (11.4s for Yelp2018, 30.1s for Gowalla on an RTX
5090 --- both far under the design budget) and cached as a fixed-size candidate pool
per user.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{One-time PPR precompute and band extraction (per dataset)}
\label{alg:ppr}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require normalized adjacency $\tilde{\mathcal{A}}$, teleport $c$, iterations $n_{\mathrm{iter}}$,
band $(p_{lo},p_{hi})$, pool size $P$, chunk size $C$
\Ensure candidate pool $\mathrm{POOL}\in\mathbb{Z}^{|U|\times P}$, degrees $d\in\mathbb{Z}^{|U|}$
\For{each user chunk $\mathcal{C}$ of size $C$}
  \State $X \gets E_{\mathcal{C}}$ \Comment{one-hot personalization on user nodes}
  \For{$k=1$ to $n_{\mathrm{iter}}$}
     \State $X \gets (1-c)\,\tilde{\mathcal{A}}\,X + c\,E_{\mathcal{C}}$ \Comment{sparse mm, geometric convergence}
  \EndFor
  \State $S \gets$ item-node rows of $X$ \Comment{PPR scores over items}
  \State $S[u,j]\gets -\infty$ for all $j\in N_u$ \Comment{exclude observed positives}
  \State $(\mathrm{vals},\mathrm{idx})\gets \mathrm{topk}(S,\;\lceil p_{hi}|I|\rceil)$; sort per row
  \State $B_u \gets \mathrm{idx}[\,\lfloor p_{lo}|I|\rfloor:\,]$ per user \Comment{drop top-$p_{lo}$}
  \State $\mathrm{POOL}[\mathcal{C}]\gets$ sample $P$ items from each $B_u$ (uniform)
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{S3 degree-gated curriculum negative sampling (per epoch, vectorized over all $|O|$ edges)}
\label{alg:sampler}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require edge users $r\in\mathbb{Z}^{|O|}$, epoch $t$, warm-up $T_w$, slope $a$,
midpoint $d_{\mathrm{mid}}$, pool $\mathrm{POOL}$, degrees $d$, RNG
\Ensure negatives $\mathrm{neg}\in\mathbb{Z}^{|O|}$
\State $g_u \gets \sigma\!\big(a[\log(1{+}d_u)-\log(1{+}d_{\mathrm{mid}})]\big)$ \Comment{gate; precomputed once}
\State $s \gets \min(1,\,t/T_w)$; \quad $\alpha \gets s\cdot g_{r}$ \Comment{per-edge mixture weight}
\State $m \gets \big(\mathrm{RNG.uniform}(|O|) < \alpha\big)$ \Comment{PPR-branch mask}
\State $\mathrm{neg}_{\mathrm{ppr}} \gets \mathrm{POOL}[\,r,\;\mathrm{RNG.int}(0,P,|O|)\,]$ \Comment{pool excludes positives}
\State $\mathrm{neg}_{\mathrm{uni}} \gets \mathrm{RNG.int}(0,|I|,|O|)$;\quad resample entries colliding with $N_r$
\State $\mathrm{neg} \gets \mathrm{where}(m,\;\mathrm{neg}_{\mathrm{ppr}},\;\mathrm{neg}_{\mathrm{uni}})$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Illustrative implementation}
\label{sec:code-snippets}
Listing~\ref{lst:sampler} shows the core of the vectorized per-epoch sampler
(\texttt{TrnData.neg\_sampling} in \texttt{third\_party/LightGCL/utils.py}),
implementing Algorithm~\ref{alg:sampler} directly: the degree gate \texttt{base\_u}
is precomputed once at construction time (line 2), and each epoch computes a
per-edge mixture probability and vectorized branch mask (lines 6--10) rather than
looping in Python. Listing~\ref{lst:stability} shows the overflow-safe InfoNCE
term (\S\ref{sec:stability}), where \texttt{torch.logsumexp} replaces a naive
\texttt{log(exp(x).sum())} that overflows once embedding norms grow unbounded.

\begin{lstlisting}[style=py,float=t,caption={Degree-gated curriculum sampler: gate precompute (constructor) and per-epoch vectorized sampling.},label={lst:sampler}]
# constructor (called once): precompute the degree gate
if neg_mode == 'S3':
    d_mid = gate_dmid if gate_dmid is not None else float(np.median(deg))
    self.base_u = 1.0 / (1.0 + np.exp(-gate_a * (np.log1p(deg) - np.log1p(d_mid))))

# neg_sampling(epoch): called once per epoch, vectorized over all |O| edges
def neg_sampling(self, epoch=0):
    E, rows = len(self.rows), self.rows
    if self.neg_mode == 'S0':
        self.negs = self._sample_uniform(rows); return

    s = min(1.0, epoch / self.Tw) if self.Tw > 0 else 1.0
    if self.neg_mode == 'S1':
        alpha_edge = np.ones(E)
    elif self.neg_mode == 'S2':
        alpha_edge = np.full(E, s * self.alpha_bar)
    elif self.neg_mode == 'S3':
        alpha_edge = s * self.base_u[rows]          # per-edge mixture weight

    use_ppr = self.rng.random(E) < alpha_edge        # vectorized branch mask
    neg = np.zeros(E, dtype=np.int32)
    if use_ppr.any():
        ppr_rows = rows[use_ppr]
        col_idx = self.rng.integers(0, self.pool_size, size=len(ppr_rows))
        neg[use_ppr] = self.ppr_pool[ppr_rows, col_idx]
    if (~use_ppr).any():
        neg[~use_ppr] = self._sample_uniform(rows[~use_ppr])
    self.negs = neg
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{lstlisting}[style=py,float=t,caption={Overflow-safe InfoNCE contrastive term (\S\ref{sec:stability}): \texttt{logsumexp} instead of \texttt{log(exp(x).sum())}.},label={lst:stability}]
# logsumexp instead of log(exp(.).sum()): the raw exp() overflows to inf once
# embedding norms grow (no bound over long unregularized training), and inf/nan
# then poisons the total loss even when lambda_1=0 (0 * nan = nan).
neg_score = torch.logsumexp(G_u_norm[uids] @ E_u_norm.T / self.temp, dim=1).mean()
neg_score += torch.logsumexp(G_i_norm[iids] @ E_i_norm.T / self.temp, dim=1).mean()
pos_score = (torch.clamp((G_u_norm[uids] * E_u_norm[uids]).sum(1) / self.temp, -5.0, 5.0)).mean() \
          + (torch.clamp((G_i_norm[iids] * E_i_norm[iids]).sum(1) / self.temp, -5.0, 5.0)).mean()
loss_s = -pos_score + neg_score
\end{lstlisting}

\section{Experimental setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Baselines.} MF-BPR, LightGCN, SimGCL, and LightGCL. MF-BPR and LightGCN are
obtained as ablations of the same LightGCL codebase (\texttt{--gnn\_layer 0 --lambda1 0}
and \texttt{--lambda1 0} respectively; see \S\ref{sec:method}). SimGCL~\cite{simgcl} is
implemented separately (\texttt{scripts/baselines/simgcl.py}) since it requires
noise-perturbation-based contrastive augmentation rather than an SVD view; it uses its
own (unmodified, uniform) negative sampler, matching its official design.

\paragraph{Ablation.} S0/S1/S2/S3 as defined in \S\ref{sec:method}, all on LightGCL, on
Yelp2018.

\paragraph{Protocol.} All models: $d=64$, 2 GNN layers, 100 epochs, Adam, following
LightGCL's default architecture hyperparameters unless noted below. \emph{Screening}
(single seed) swept the PPR band $(p_{lo}, p_{hi}) \in \{(0.05,0.15), (0.02,0.10),
(0.10,0.20)\}$ for S1, selecting the best band $(0.02,0.10)$ for S2/S3; $\bar\alpha \in
\{0.3, 0.5\}$ for S2; and $(a, T_w) \in \{(1.0,10),(1.5,10),(1.0,5)\}$ for S3 --- 9
LightGCL-ablation runs plus MF-BPR/LightGCN screening, 11 runs total, all on a single
RTX 5090, none diverged (see \S\ref{sec:stability}). \emph{Final} numbers re-run the best
band/hyperparameters for S0, S2 ($\bar\alpha=0.3$), and S3 ($a=1.0, T_w=10$) with two
additional seeds (3 seeds total per config, with per-user metrics saved for the
degree-bucket table), and MF-BPR/LightGCN/S1-best with one additional seed (2 seeds
total); SimGCL was run fresh at 2 seeds. Time budget did not permit a Gowalla run or the
secondary SVD-rank ablation from the proposal; both are noted as future work.

\paragraph{Implementation notes / stability fixes.}
\label{sec:stability}
Two numerical-stability bugs in the unmodified LightGCL reference code caused training to
diverge to \texttt{NaN} on long (100-epoch) runs, discovered while reproducing the
MF-BPR and LightGCN ablations:
\begin{enumerate}
\item The BPR loss computed \texttt{sigmoid(x).log()} directly, which returns $-\infty$
once score margins grow large enough to saturate the sigmoid in float32. We replaced it
with the numerically-stable \texttt{F.logsigmoid(x)} and added a defensive
\texttt{clamp(x, -20, 20)}, which is a no-op for well-behaved training but blocks
runaway growth (the clamp's zero gradient outside the bounds also acts as an implicit
stabilizer).
\item The InfoNCE-style contrastive loss computed \texttt{log(exp(x).sum())} without the
log-sum-exp trick; once embeddings grow, \texttt{exp(x)} overflows to \texttt{inf}, and
\texttt{log(inf)=inf} poisons the total loss \emph{even when $\lambda_1=0$} (i.e. even
for the LightGCN ablation, which does not use this term for anything but still computes
it), since $0 \times \mathrm{NaN} = \mathrm{NaN}$. We replaced it with
\texttt{torch.logsumexp}, mathematically identical for finite inputs but overflow-safe.
\end{enumerate}
We also added global gradient-norm clipping (max\_norm=5) and, since the reference code
has no seed control and its \texttt{--decay} flag is parsed but never applied to the
optimizer, added explicit \texttt{--seed} plumbing (torch + numpy) for reproducibility.
After these fixes, we stress-tested LightGCN for a full 100 epochs and observed
\emph{zero} \texttt{NaN} occurrences (final Recall@20=0.0898); before the fixes, the same
configuration reproducibly diverged between epochs 9 and 93 depending on random
initialization. MF-BPR additionally needed a lower learning rate and higher $L_2$ weight
($\mathrm{lr}=10^{-4}$, $\lambda_2=10^{-5}$ vs. the repository's LightGCN-tuned
$\mathrm{lr}=10^{-3}$, $\lambda_2=10^{-7}$) since it has no graph-smoothing to bound
embedding scale. These fixes are orthogonal to our sampler contribution but were
necessary for \emph{any} of the baselines to produce a trustworthy 100-epoch number, and
we release them as part of this codebase.

Four patterns emerge, all consistent with the method's design logic and visible in
Figure~\ref{fig:sweep}. \textbf{(i) Band width.} Among the pure-PPR S1 runs, the
narrowest/hardest band $(0.02,0.10)$ is the only one to reach the uniform baseline at
seed $0$ ($0.1006$ vs.\ $0.1002$), while the ``safest'' wide band $(0.10,0.20)$ is worst
($0.0990$). This is initially counter-intuitive --- one might expect the safer band to
reduce false negatives --- but it is coherent: items in the $10$--$20$th percentile of
PPR are neither false-negative risks \emph{nor} informative hard negatives; they are
mediocre mid-difficulty items that add noise without gradient signal. The hardest band
concentrates on genuinely informative items, and false-negative damage is then better
controlled by the \emph{curriculum and gate} (S2/S3) than by widening the band. This
validates selecting $(0.02,0.10)$ for the mixture variants. \textbf{(ii) Mixture
weight.} S2 is essentially flat in $\bar\alpha$ over $\{0.3,0.5\}$ ($0.1012$ vs.\
$0.1011$ Recall@20), with the lower weight marginally ahead --- more hard negatives
past a modest fraction neither helps nor clearly hurts. \textbf{(iii) Gate slope.} A
gentler gate ($a{=}1.0$) matches or beats the steeper $a{=}1.5$ on NDCG@20 ($0.0873$
vs.\ $0.0869$), i.e.\ sharpening the sparse/active contrast does not help.

$T_w{=}5$ ($0.1009$ vs.\ $0.1007$ Recall@20), consistent with the false-negative
hypothesis that hard negatives are harmful early and should be introduced slowly.

The single most important quantitative observation, however, is the \emph{scale} of
all these effects. The entire span of the eight S1--S3 configurations is
$0.0990$--$0.1012$ Recall@20, a range of $0.0022$; the span of the six mixture
(S2/S3) configurations that all beat the baseline is just $0.1007$--$0.1012$, a
range of $0.0005$. That $0.0005$ window is \emph{smaller than the across-seed
standard deviation} we measure for a single fixed configuration in the finals
($\sigma\!\approx\!0.0006$--$0.0008$ Recall@20; Table~\ref{tab:main-results}). In
other words, once a curriculum ramp is present, no hyperparameter choice within our
grid moves the model by more than seed noise. This is the empirical backdrop
against which the S2-vs-S3 comparison in \S\ref{sec:discussion} must be read.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

Returning to the research question --- does a hard-negative curriculum modulated by user
activity improve accuracy and long-tail robustness without increasing false-negative
bias? --- our results support the first half more clearly than the second. A curriculum
mixture of uniform and PPR hard negatives (S2 or S3) improves both overall accuracy and
every degree bucket over pure uniform sampling (S0), and pure hard-negative sampling
without any curriculum or protection (S1) is the one variant that backfires, exactly as
the false-negative-bias hypothesis predicts. But the \emph{degree-specific} gating that
is this project's distinguishing contribution over a simpler fixed-ramp mixture does not
show a measurable edge in our experiments, including specifically in the tail bucket
where we expected it to matter most.

\subsection{Why does the degree gate not beat a flat mixture?}
\label{sec:whyS3}

The central negative finding --- S3's degree gate yields no measurable gain over the
degree-agnostic S2, even in the tail bucket --- admits four concrete, non-exclusive
explanations grounded in the method and the data of \S\ref{sec:sensitivity}. We could
not adjudicate between them within the compute budget, but each makes a testable
prediction.

\textbf{H1: The gate's dynamic range is too narrow for Yelp's degree distribution.}
This is the explanation the data most directly support. The tertile thresholds ($20$
and $33$ training interactions) show that the bulk of Yelp users are compressed into a
narrow degree band, and the gate operates in \emph{log}-degree with midpoint at the
median. Substituting representative degrees into
$g_u=\sigma(a[\log(1{+}d_u)-\log(1{+}d_{\mathrm{mid}})])$ with $a{=}1.0$ and
$d_{\mathrm{mid}}$ at the median (between the $20$ and $33$ tertile cutoffs) gives gate
values of roughly $0.30$ for a sparse tail user ($d\!\approx\!10$), $0.5$ at the median,
and $0.63$ for a mid/head user ($d\!\approx\!33$) --- a spread of only about $\pm0.16$
around $0.5$ across the central mass of users.\footnote{These gate values are
deterministic consequences of the stated formula, $a{=}1.0$, and the median lying
between the reported tertile cutoffs; they are illustrative of the gate's shape, not
measured quantities. The exact numbers shift slightly with the precise median.} Only
pathologically sparse or extremely active users reach the $0.1$/$0.9$ extremes the gate
was drawn to exploit, and those users are a small fraction of the interaction mass.
Consequently S3's \emph{effective} mixture weight is approximately a flat
$\approx\!0.5$ for most edges --- i.e.\ S3 behaves almost exactly like S2 at
$\bar\alpha\!\approx\!0.5$. Table~\ref{tab:screening} confirms the prediction this
makes: S2 at $\bar\alpha{=}0.5$ ($0.1011$) and best S3 ($0.1009$) are indeed nearly
identical, and both sit just below the better-tuned S2 at $\bar\alpha{=}0.3$
($0.1012$). Under this account the degree gate is not \emph{wrong}; it is simply
near-degenerate on this dataset, collapsing toward a flat mixture whose implied weight
($\approx\!0.5$) happens to be marginally worse than the tuned S2 weight ($0.3$) ---
which explains both why S3 fails to beat S2 and why it slightly trails the best S2.

\textbf{H2: The effect is smaller than the seed noise.} As quantified in
\S\ref{sec:sensitivity}, the S2-vs-S3 gap is $\le 0.0006$ Recall@20 while
per-configuration std is $0.0006$--$0.0008$ over only 2--3 seeds. Even in the tail
bucket the S2/S3 difference ($0.1017$ vs.\ $0.1011$ Recall@20) is of the same
magnitude as the tail-bucket std. A properly powered comparison would need many more
seeds; with the current sample the two methods are plausibly statistically
indistinguishable, and the observed S2 $>$ S3 ordering could reverse. This does not
rescue the gate --- an effect that requires dozens of seeds to detect is not a
practically useful contribution --- but it does mean we cannot claim the gate
\emph{hurts}, only that we cannot detect it helping.

\textbf{H3: The gate is under-tuned relative to S2.} The comparison is mildly unfair:
S2's single scalar $\bar\alpha$ was swept over $\{0.3,0.5\}$, whereas S3's two-parameter
gate $(a,d_{\mathrm{mid}})$ was explored only over two slopes at a fixed median
midpoint, in a single-seed pass. H1 suggests the more promising untried move is not a
steeper slope but a \emph{shifted or rescaled} midpoint --- e.g.\ setting
$d_{\mathrm{mid}}$ well above the median and/or increasing $a$ sharply --- so that the
gate actually reaches its protective regime for the sparse users it targets, rather
than hovering near $0.5$ for everyone. We did not run this, and flag it as the most
direct follow-up to test H1.

\textbf{H4: Degree is a coarse proxy for false-negative risk.} The gate assumes sparse
users need protection because their unobserved interactions are more ambiguous, but
false-negative risk plausibly also depends on item popularity, local neighborhood
density, and the coherence of a user's taste (two users at the same degree can have a
tightly clustered or a broad, noisy interaction history). A scalar function of $d_u$
alone cannot distinguish these cases, which is consistent with S2's slight edge over S3
persisting even within the tail bucket, where H1's compressed-range argument should be
weakest. This suggests that if a gate is to help, the conditioning signal may need to be
richer than raw degree --- e.g.\ combining degree with a popularity or local-clustering
estimate --- rather than only refining the degree gate's slope or midpoint (H3).

Taken together, H1 is the explanation with the most direct empirical support and it
subsumes part of H3: on a degree distribution as compressed as Yelp2018's, a
log-degree sigmoid centred at the median cannot differentiate users strongly enough to
beat a well-chosen flat mixture. This sharpens rather than retracts the project's
thesis --- the \emph{curriculum} clearly helps (S2/S3 $>$ S0, S1 backfires exactly as
predicted), while the \emph{degree-conditioning} needs either a sparser dataset
(Gowalla, at $0.045\%$ vs.\ Yelp's $0.188\%$ density, spreads users over a wider degree
range) or a more aggressively parameterized gate to demonstrate its intended benefit.

We also did not anticipate SimGCL's large margin over every LightGCL variant
(Table~\ref{tab:main-results}); since we did not tune SimGCL's hyperparameters for this
dataset, we cannot yet attribute this to model quality vs. tuning, and flag it as the
most important open question for follow-up work, ahead of further tuning our own method.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

We list the limitations that most qualify our conclusions, in decreasing order of how
much they affect the headline claims.

\textbf{Statistical power.} Our strongest quantitative caveat is that the effects we
study are small relative to seed variance. Finals use 2--3 seeds; the S2-vs-S3 gap and
even several baseline gaps are of the same order as the measured standard deviation
(\S\ref{sec:sensitivity}). We therefore report all numbers with std and avoid
significance claims we cannot support; a publishable version would require $\ge\!10$
seeds and a paired test.

\textbf{Single dataset.} All conclusions are drawn from Yelp2018 alone. The degree
gate's near-degeneracy (H1, \S\ref{sec:whyS3}) is specifically a property of Yelp's
compressed degree distribution; whether the gate helps on a sparser graph is untested.
We precomputed PPR for Gowalla (Table~\ref{tab:dataset-stats}) but could not run
training within the compute budget, so we cannot claim cross-dataset generality.

\textbf{Baseline tuning asymmetry.} SimGCL's large margin over all LightGCL variants is
unexplained. We did not tune SimGCL's noise scale $\epsilon$ or contrastive weight for
Yelp2018, nor did we re-tune LightGCL beyond its repository defaults, so we cannot
attribute the gap to model quality versus tuning. Our contribution is an
\emph{intra-LightGCL} ablation (S0--S3 share every hyperparameter except the sampler);
cross-model rankings in Table~\ref{tab:main-results} should be read as context, not as
a controlled comparison.

\textbf{Fixed PPR construction.} PPR is computed once with fixed teleport $c{=}0.15$ and
$30$ power iterations on the symmetric-normalized adjacency, and the hard-negative band
is frozen for all of training. Unlike model-adaptive samplers (DNS, SRNS), our hardness
signal is static and cannot track which items the current model actually confuses; a
stale band may include items the model has already learned to rank correctly. The SVD
rank $q$ of LightGCL's contrastive view was also left at its default and never ablated.

\textbf{Metric and protocol scope.} We report Recall@20/NDCG@20 under full ranking only.
We do not measure diversity, coverage, novelty, or calibration, so we cannot
characterize how hard-negative mining reshapes the \emph{distribution} of
recommendations --- directly relevant to the impact concerns below.

\section{Broader impact and ethics}
\label{sec:impact}

\textbf{Popularity and feedback-loop amplification.} Hard-negative mining and
recommender feedback loops interact in a way that deserves explicit caution. PPR
proximity correlates with item popularity: popular items accumulate PPR mass and are
disproportionately likely to enter the hard-negative band. Training against popular
items as negatives can suppress their scores (a debiasing effect) \emph{or}, if the
band is dominated by unobserved-positive popular items, distort the ranking in
harder-to-predict ways. More broadly, any change that sharpens the decision boundary
risks amplifying existing exposure bias through the standard feedback loop, in which a
model trained on interactions it previously induced further concentrates
exposure~\cite{biassurvey}. Our degree gate is, from this angle, a partial
mitigation --- it withholds aggressive hard negatives from exactly the sparse users
most vulnerable to mis-ranking --- but we did not measure popularity or exposure
distributions, so we cannot claim a net debiasing effect and would caution against
deploying the sampler as one without such measurement.

\textbf{False negatives as fairness risk.} The false-negative mechanism central to this
paper has an equity dimension: treating a PPR-proximate unobserved item as a negative
penalizes items a user might genuinely like but has not yet encountered, and this
penalty falls hardest on cold-start users and long-tail items with sparse evidence.
This is the harm the degree gate is designed to bound, and we regard protecting sparse
users as an ethical as well as an accuracy objective; our tail-bucket results (no bucket
is made worse than uniform sampling) are the relevant evidence, though limited to one
dataset.

\textbf{Compute and reproducibility.} PPR precompute is a one-time cost (11.4s Yelp /
30.1s Gowalla on one GPU) and the per-epoch sampler adds $<\!1$s, so the method's
marginal energy cost over standard LightGCL training is negligible; the dominant cost
is the training itself, and our full study is a modest number of single-GPU runs. We
nonetheless note that hyperparameter sweeps of contrastive recommenders are
energy-consuming at scale, and our finding that the entire mixture-hyperparameter grid
moves accuracy by less than seed noise (\S\ref{sec:sensitivity}) is itself an argument
\emph{against} extensive sweeping here. All data is a public benchmark of business
check-ins with no direct personal identifiers beyond anonymized user ids; no new data
was collected.

\section{Conclusion and future work}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We implemented and evaluated a degree-aware curriculum hard-negative sampler for
LightGCL's BPR loss, changing only the negative proposal distribution and leaving the
SVD-contrastive branch provably untouched. On Yelp2018 we confirmed, on real data and
across seeds, the two halves of the motivating hypothesis that do hold: pure PPR
hard-negative sampling without protection (S1) is the single variant that underperforms
uniform sampling, exactly as the false-negative-bias mechanism predicts, and adding a
curriculum ramp (S2/S3) recovers and exceeds the uniform baseline on overall metrics
and in \emph{every} degree bucket, so a hard-negative curriculum can help sparse users
without net harm. The distinguishing component of our full method --- degree-conditioned
gating --- did not, however, produce a measurable gain over a simpler degree-agnostic
mixture. Our analysis (\S\ref{sec:whyS3}) attributes this primarily to the gate becoming
near-degenerate on Yelp's compressed degree distribution, where a log-degree sigmoid
centred at the median collapses toward a flat $\approx\!0.5$ mixture for the bulk of
users. We regard the honest reporting of this partial result, together with two
numerical-stability fixes to the reference LightGCL code (\S\ref{sec:stability}) that
are useful independent of our contribution, as the study's most reliable outputs.

\textbf{Future work} follows directly from the limitations and the H1--H3 analysis.
\emph{(1) Sparser data.} The most direct test of the degree gate is Gowalla, whose
$0.045\%$ density (versus Yelp's $0.188\%$) spreads users over a far wider degree
range; PPR is already precomputed (Table~\ref{tab:dataset-stats}), so this is a
training-time-only extension, and H1 predicts a larger S3-over-S2 gap there.
\emph{(2) Re-parameterized gate.} H1/H3 suggest the untried lever is the gate
\emph{midpoint and range}, not its slope: setting $d_{\mathrm{mid}}$ above the median
(and/or normalizing degree by percentile rather than raw log-degree) would push the
gate into its protective regime for the sparse users it targets, rather than hovering
near $0.5$ for everyone. \emph{(3) Adaptive hardness.} Replacing the static PPR band
with a model-aware signal --- score-conditioned negatives as in DNS~\cite{dns} or
variance-based false-negative filtering as in SRNS~\cite{srns}, gated by degree as
here --- would let the hardness track what the model actually confuses, addressing the
stale-band limitation. \emph{(4) SVD-rank ablation.} The proposal's planned sweep of
the contrastive view's SVD rank $q$ remains open and would clarify how much of
LightGCL's headroom is in the contrastive branch versus the ranking term we modify.
\emph{(5) Powered evaluation and the SimGCL gap.} A many-seed, paired-test evaluation
is needed both to settle the S2-vs-S3 question and, more importantly, to determine
whether SimGCL's large advantage reflects genuine model quality or untuned LightGCL
hyperparameters --- the single most consequential open question raised by our results.

1{,}561{,}406 interactions (0.130\% density), sourced from the original LightGCN paper's

no extra preprocessing is required") has different statistics, reported in
Table~\ref{tab:dataset-stats}. We use the shipped data as-is, per the proposal's own
methodology, and report the measured statistics rather than the proposal draft's.
